GNU FSDG and RYF: Freedom, Firmware, and Hardware Reality

By David Uhden Collado - Updated July 1, 2026

GNU's FSDG and FSF's RYF remain influential freedom standards, but I argue that they mishandle firmware, underweight modern hardware dependencies, and leave a gap between formal compliance and practical user control.

My critique of FSDG and RYF is not a rejection of software freedom itself. I am challenging their design boundaries: what counts as acceptable freedom today, how exceptions are applied, and whether the current model pushes vendors and communities toward real technical emancipation or merely formal alignment.

What the Criteria Actually Require

The GNU Free System Distribution Guidelines (FSDG) require endorsed distributions to remove nonfree software, including nonfree firmware blobs, and to avoid steering users to nonfree repositories or documentation. This creates a high bar centered on strict software curation.

RYF applies similar principles to hardware products, demanding that product software be free and installable in modified form. However, the published criteria include an exception for software running on certain secondary embedded processors where post-purchase installation is not intended[1]. That carve-out is one of the most debated points.

Firmware: Where Theory Meets Physical Devices

Modern laptops, servers, and peripherals depend on layered firmware: Wi-Fi chips, storage controllers, GPUs, embedded controllers, and platform microcode. In many cases, those components are required for baseline functionality, and alternatives are either unavailable or immature.

I argue that the current criteria can create a contradictory outcome. FSDG strongly rejects nonfree firmware in distributions, while RYF allows exceptions at some hardware layers. Supporters see principled consistency here; I see asymmetry: strict in package policy, permissive where hardware is hardest to audit.

Adoption Pressure and Distribution Policy

The friction is visible in distribution governance. Debian's 2022 General Resolution on non-free firmware and subsequent Debian 12 implementation prioritized installability on mainstream hardware, with explicit user-facing disclosure and controls. GNU, by contrast, treats this direction as noncompliant with free-distro endorsement standards and cites it when explaining non-endorsement.

My practical objection is straightforward: if a freedom standard cannot be deployed by major projects without severe hardware regressions, it risks becoming socially marginal. I recognize the ethical counterargument—lowering requirements can normalize lock-in—but the unresolved issue is still how to keep pressure for liberation without disconnecting from real deployment conditions.

The Scope Critique: Software Freedom vs. Device Freedom

I also challenge the scope. FSDG and RYF are mostly drafted around software licensing and distribution behavior. They are weaker at evaluating repairability, diagnostic openness, hardware serviceability, and anti-circumvention pressure that can block legitimate maintenance and reverse engineering.

This matters because user autonomy now depends on both code and hardware lifecycle control. Right-to-repair advocates have shown how legal and technical restrictions around embedded software can undermine ownership even when parts of the stack are open. A criteria framework that does not score those constraints can overstate practical freedom.

Conclusions

My strongest critique of FSDG and RYF is not that they are too strict in principle, but that they are uneven in effect: rigorous where projects can edit package sets, less transformative where hardware power actually concentrates.

A stronger next generation would preserve the current freedom floor, tighten exception pathways, and add explicit metrics for repair, recoverability, firmware replaceability, and user-verifiable control across the full device stack. That would shift policy from symbolic purity toward measurable freedom outcomes.

The GPL: A Tool with Paradoxes in the World of Free Software

By David Uhden Collado • Updated March 6, 2025

The GPL set out to secure software freedom, but its reciprocal restrictions can limit collaboration and create friction between open and permissive models.

The GNU General Public License (GPL) was born with the noble intention of countering the proprietary software model by guaranteeing the four essential freedoms: to run, study, share, and modify the software. However, the GPL contains contradictions and side effects that can undermine the spirit of cooperation and freedom that the community seeks to foster.

A Design with Contradictory Restrictions

The GPL was designed to prevent software under its license from being incorporated into proprietary projects. This means that if a program uses GPL-licensed code, all derived software must also be subject to the same terms. Although the intention is to protect user freedom, this requirement can limit the use of components that, under a permissive license, could be more freely integrated into heterogeneous systems.

For example, in the development of the Linux kernel, the license forces any linked component to be subject to the same restrictions, which compels developers and companies to adopt complex solutions—such as dynamic linking instead of static linking—to avoid compromising their own business models. This complexity has led, in many cases, to the license's legality being ignored in certain regions, creating uncertainty about its practical application.

The Dichotomy Between Philosophy and Practice

In theory, the GPL defends the idea that sharing is synonymous with freedom. However, imposing legal restrictions to force code contributions goes against the very spirit of cooperation. By limiting how the software can be reused, the license might prevent projects under more open licenses—such as BSD or MIT—from mutually benefiting from collaborative work.

Furthermore, there is an inherent tension: on one hand, it promotes an ethic of sharing intended to protect the end user and the community; on the other, it establishes a restrictive framework that is a form of control over how software is distributed and used. This paradox has fueled intense debates about whether the GPL truly fosters freedom or if, in some cases, it actually confines it.

The Impact on Collaboration and Business Development

Another controversial aspect is the GPL's relationship with the business world. Large corporations have found permissive licenses to be a more fertile ground for collaboration and technological development without the obligation to reveal specific changes. For instance, the development of Clang and LLVM has demonstrated that a less restrictive approach can generate significant innovations and fruitful collaborations between companies and communities.

In contrast, the rigidity of the GPL has sometimes led to strategies of “hijacking” projects, where, faced with the impossibility of freely integrating the code, some companies resort to political maneuvers or even acquiring projects to impose their own terms. This not only fragments the community but also diverts attention from the main goal: creating software that truly empowers its users.

Toward an Ethic of Sharing Without Coercion

The central critique is that freedom should not be imposed through legal restrictions that, paradoxically, limit the use of the software. The true essence of free software is based on trust in human cooperation, on the belief that if an environment of collaboration is created and users are educated, most will voluntarily contribute to the development and improvement of projects.

The challenge, therefore, is to find a balance between protecting one's work and fostering an ecosystem where contributions flow naturally, without being hindered by legal requirements that may prove counterproductive. Permissive licenses offer an alternative path, allowing for more flexible and adaptive integration that addresses both commercial and community needs.

Conclusions

Despite its noble intentions, the GPL presents limitations that hinder the true spirit of free software: open collaboration and sharing without excessive restrictions. Although its original purpose of preventing private appropriation is commendable, in practice tensions and barriers have arisen that impede a more open and fluid cooperation among developers, communities, and companies. The final reflection invites us to rethink the legal tools we use, always striving to promote an environment where freedom and collaboration are genuine, without coercions that ultimately limit the software's potential to transform society.

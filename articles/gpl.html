The GPL: Paradoxes in Free Software

By David Uhden Collado - Updated March 6, 2025

The GPL set out to secure software freedom, but its reciprocal restrictions can limit collaboration and create friction between open and permissive models.

Contents

  2. The Dichotomy Between Philosophy and Practice
  3. The Impact on Collaboration and Business Development
  4. Toward an Ethic of Sharing Without Coercion
  5. Conclusions

The GNU General Public License (GPL) was created to counter the proprietary software model by guaranteeing four essential freedoms: running, studying, sharing, and modifying software. However, the GPL also introduces contradictions and side effects that can undermine the spirit of cooperation and freedom the community seeks to foster.

A Design with Contradictory Restrictions

The GPL was designed to prevent software under its terms from being incorporated into proprietary projects. This means that if a program uses GPL-licensed code, all derivative software must also remain under those terms. Although the intention is to protect user freedom, this requirement can limit the use of components that, under a permissive license, could be integrated more freely into heterogeneous systems.

For example, in Linux kernel development, the license requires linked components to follow the same restrictions. As a result, developers and companies often adopt complex solutions—such as dynamic rather than static linking—to avoid compromising their business models. In many cases, that complexity has led to the license being ignored in certain jurisdictions, creating uncertainty about how it is applied in practice.

The Dichotomy Between Philosophy and Practice

In theory, the GPL argues that sharing and freedom are aligned. However, imposing legal restrictions to force code contributions can run against the spirit of cooperation itself. By limiting how software can be reused, the license may prevent projects under more permissive licenses—such as BSD or MIT—from benefiting reciprocally from collaborative work.

There is also an inherent tension: on one hand, the GPL promotes an ethic of sharing intended to protect end users and the community; on the other, it establishes a restrictive framework that controls how software is distributed and used. This paradox has fueled intense debate about whether the GPL truly expands freedom or, in some cases, narrows it.

The Impact on Collaboration and Business Development

Another controversial aspect is the GPL's relationship with the business world. Large corporations have often found permissive licenses to be more fertile ground for collaboration and technological development, without an obligation to disclose specific changes. For instance, the development of Clang and LLVM has shown that a less restrictive approach can produce significant innovation and fruitful collaboration between companies and communities.

By contrast, the GPL's rigidity has sometimes led to project "hijacking" strategies: when free integration is not feasible, some companies resort to political maneuvers or project acquisitions to impose their own terms. This not only fragments communities but also diverts attention from the central goal: creating software that truly empowers users.

Toward an Ethic of Sharing Without Coercion

The central critique is that freedom should not be imposed through legal restrictions that, paradoxically, limit software use. The essence of free software rests on trust in human cooperation: if collaborative environments are built and users are educated, most will contribute voluntarily to project development and improvement.

The challenge, then, is to balance protection of one's work with an ecosystem in which contributions flow naturally, without legal requirements that may prove counterproductive. Permissive licenses offer an alternative path, enabling more flexible, adaptive integration that addresses both commercial and community needs.

Conclusions

Despite its noble intentions, the GPL presents limitations that can hinder the core spirit of free software: open collaboration and sharing without excessive restrictions. Although its original purpose of preventing private appropriation is commendable, practical tensions and barriers have emerged that impede more open, fluid cooperation among developers, communities, and companies. The broader takeaway is an invitation to rethink our legal tools and keep promoting environments where freedom and collaboration are genuine, without coercion that ultimately limits software's potential to transform society.
